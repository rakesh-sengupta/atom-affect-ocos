Numerical cognition is a cornerstone of human intelligence, yet the
mechanisms by which emotional state modulates number perception remain
unspecified. Walsh's (2003) \emph{A Theory of Magnitude} (ATOM) proposes
that time, space, and number share a common parietal substrate in the
Intraparietal Sulcus (IPS), but the theory does not address the role of
affect. We address this gap using the On-Centre Off-Surround (OCOS)
recurrent neural network model of numerosity perception and compare three
hypothetical pathways through which the amygdala-to-IPS projection could
modulate number sense: (A) arousal scales lateral inhibition $\beta$ and
valence scales gain $g$ (ATOM-extended); (B) affect scales self-excitation
$\alpha$ only, with $\beta$ fixed (neuromodulatory); and (C) affect
delivers a transient input salience shock (orienting-response). We evaluate
all three against five psychophysical constraints drawn from the numerosity
literature across 90,000 Monte Carlo simulation runs. Architecture~A
satisfies 4.9/5.0 constraints and is the only model in which the
subitizing/estimation boundary shift is specifically beta-mediated. High-
arousal affective conditions produce significant overestimation for $N \geq
5$ (Threat: $t = +15.70$, $p < .001$; Excitement: $t = +11.34$, $p <
.001$), while low-arousal negative affect produces underestimation (Boredom:
$t = -17.27$, $p < .001$). The model generates falsifiable predictions for
an empirical companion study and a clinical simulation of mathematical
anxiety as chronically elevated~$\beta$. These findings contribute to the
emerging intersection of affective computing, computational neuroscience,
and cognitive technology.
